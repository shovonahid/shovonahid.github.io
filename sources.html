References & Citations

Sources

All research, interviews, and materials referenced in this project.

This project draws on three published sources and one primary interview. Each source is listed below with a brief note on how it contributed to the argument. Full citation information is included for reference.

  • 1

    Artificial Intelligence and Its Impact on Education

    Klimova, B. & Pikhart, M.

    This study examined the psychological and social effects of AI use on students. The researchers found that over-reliance on AI tools can diminish interpersonal skills, reduce emotional intelligence, and lead to increased anxiety. They also argued that students are not being taught to critically evaluate what AI generates which points to a major gap in how schools approach AI education.

    Peer-Reviewed Study Social & Emotional Impact
  • 2

    OpenAI on AI in Education — Interview / Public Statement

    Belsky, Leah — Head of Education, OpenAI

    Leah Belsky's observations came from public commentary on how institutions responded to AI. She noted that schools focused on policing student use of AI rather than redesigning how they assess and teach. Her perspective provided an institutional lens on why the educational system's initial response to AI was misaligned with what students actually needed.

    Industry Expert Institutional Response
  • 3

    Generative AI and Student Learning Outcomes: A Meta-Analysis

    Zhu, C., Liu, Q., & Zhao, L.

    This meta-analysis synthesized findings from multiple studies on generative AI in educational settings. The researchers found that AI produces a statistically significant but small overall effect on learning outcomes. The most important finding was that interventions lasting ten or more weeks produced nearly twice the impact of shorter engagements showing that sustained use is essential for real benefit.

    Meta-Analysis Learning Outcomes
  • 4

    Student Interview: AI Use in Engineering Coursework

    Daniel — Engineering Student (Primary Interview)

    Daniel is an engineering student who uses AI tools regularly for coursework. In the interview he described using AI as a step-by-step problem-solving guide rather than as a shortcut to finished answers. His account illustrated the distinction between thoughtful AI use and dependency and served as a real-world counterpoint to the concerns raised in the academic literature.

    Primary Interview Student Perspective

A Note on Sources

How These Sources Work Together

Each source in this project was chosen to represent a different angle on the same question. Klimova and Pikhart represent the academic warning about what unchecked AI use can do to students. Leah Belsky represents the institutional failure to respond thoughtfully. Zhu, Liu, and Zhao represent the quantitative evidence for what kind of AI use actually works. And Daniel's interview grounds all of it in the real experience of a real student.

Together these sources show that the problem is not AI itself. The problem is the absence of intentional guidance around how to use it. That is the argument this project makes and each source contributes a different piece of the evidence for it.
