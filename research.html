Evidence Based Findings

What the Research Says

Three sources. Three angles on the same problem. Here is what they found.

AI is already reshaping how students learn. But not all uses of AI are equal and the research makes that very clear. Below are the key findings from the three sources at the center of this project.

01

Over Reliance Can Hurt Students Socially and Emotionally

Klimova and Pikhart found that over reliance on AI can weaken students’ interpersonal skills, emotional intelligence, and ability to think independently.

02

Schools Responded to AI the Wrong Way

Leah Belsky argued that schools focused too much on policing AI instead of redesigning teaching and assessment for a new era of learning.

03

Long Term Use Is What Actually Helps

Zhu, Liu, and Zhao found that students benefit most from AI when it is used consistently over long periods rather than for quick one time tasks.

Research One

The Warning About Over Reliance

Students are turning to AI more than ever and researchers are starting to see the effects. AI tools are now part of daily student life ranging from homework help to emotional support. The shift is happening fast and most schools still do not have clear guidelines for how students should be using these tools in healthy ways.

"Educational approaches should highlight the need to critically assess AI generated content, compare it with insights from human sources, and recognize AI's limitations."

— Klimova & Pikhart

Critical thinking is the skill that is quietly disappearing from classrooms. Not because students are careless but because AI makes that kind of thinking feel unnecessary. When any answer is one click away the habit of questioning and analyzing starts to fade.

This is why how educators teach AI matters just as much as whether they teach it at all. The researchers did not argue for banning AI. They argued for building the capacity to question it.

Research Two

Schools Got Off on the Wrong Foot

When AI first appeared in classrooms the response from a lot of institutions was to police it. Detect it. Punish students for using it. Leah Belsky who leads education at OpenAI observed that many schools focused on catching students rather than sitting down and asking how assessment itself should be redesigned.

Schools spent energy protecting the old system instead of adapting to the new one. When the focus becomes punishment instead of progress students are the ones who end up losing.

"Institutions got off onto the wrong foot with AI in the classroom by policing its use rather than actually sitting there and figuring out how to redesign the way we assess students."

— Leah Belsky, Head of Education, OpenAI

The conversation needs to shift away from what students should not be doing and toward what schools should start doing differently. That means rethinking assignments, assessments, and what it actually means to demonstrate learning in an age where information is everywhere.

Research Three

The Data on Learning Outcomes

A large meta-analysis conducted by Zhu, Liu, and Zhao pulled together findings from many studies to measure how much AI actually affects student learning. The results were clear: generative AI does have a meaningful effect on learning outcomes but it is a small effect overall. What made the biggest difference was how long students engaged with AI tools.

Interventions that lasted ten weeks or more produced nearly double the impact of shorter ones. In other words AI does not flip a switch and suddenly improve learning. It works slowly and steadily when students engage with it consistently over time rather than reaching for it right before a deadline.

The problem is that most classroom AI use is short. A single assignment here. A quick session there. That is not how it actually helps. When AI is treated like a convenience tool the research shows the benefits shrink dramatically.
